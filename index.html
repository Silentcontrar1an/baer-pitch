Section 01  ·  Investment Thesis

A Defense Contractor Disguised as an Aerial Firefighter

What people fail to understand about Bridger Aerospace is that they are paid under the same framework as a defense contractor — IDIQ agreements guarantee fixed daily standby revenue whether or not a single fire burns.

Bridger Aerospace is not a seasonal firefighting company. It is a government-contracted aviation services business operating under the IDIQ model, where 46% of revenue accrues on a fixed daily standby basis — $42,000 per day per CL-415 Super Scooper — regardless of fire volume. The government pays Bridger to have aircraft positioned and ready. Flight hours dispatched at $13,500 per hour are incremental on top of that guaranteed base. This structure explains how Bridger grew organic revenue from $88.5M in FY2024 to $108.8M in FY2025 — up 23% — during a fire season that ran 32% below the 20-year mean. Total reported revenue of $122.8M includes $14.0M of non-recurring return-to-service work on the two Spanish scoopers acquired through the MAB Funding partnership; the underlying business is growing faster than the headline number suggests.

The company owns 8 Bombardier CL-415EAF Super Scooper aircraft — the only turbine-powered amphibious water-scooping tanker ever built at scale, production permanently closed in 2015. As of March 10, 2026, De Havilland Canada's DHC-515 — the sole announced successor — has no certification date and no delivery timeline; the manufacturer recently joined the forward fuselage on the first prototype. Approximately 110 turbine variants of the CL-415 were ever produced. Roughly 12 are in private hands. Bridger holds 8 of them.

The thesis rests on four premises. First, the wildfire structural trend is real: the 10-year average of acres burned is 7.55 million — 10% above the prior decade — and federal suppression budgets have compounded at 9.2% CAGR from $900M in 2009 to $3.2B in 2023. Second, supply cannot respond: CL-415 production is permanently closed, the DHC-515 is years from first delivery, and C-130H conversions take 2+ years per airframe. Third, demand is demonstrably unmet: unfulfilled fixed-wing requests rose 83% year-over-year to 1,048 in 2024, with 48% of scooper requests going unfulfilled. Fourth, the capital structure is approaching a resolution event: $407M in accreted preferred held by Bain Capital accretes at ~$27M per year and will not compound indefinitely.

At $2.06, the market prices Bridger at a multiple consistent with Canaccord's stale $50M estimate — predating the two Spanish scooper additions, the $100M DDTL facility, and a record 2025 task order year. The company's own FY2026 guidance midpoint of $57.5M is already 17% above that number. The author's model, built from 8-scooper fleet economics and CEO-verified unit economics of $6–8M gross profit per aircraft, implies $65–75M EBITDA — a number the fleet can mechanically produce.

Key distinction: FY2025 reported revenue of $122.8M includes $14.0M of non-recurring return-to-service work on Spanish scoopers via MAB Funding. Organic revenue was $108.8M (+23% YoY). The 2026 guidance of $135–145M represents +24–33% growth on the organic base — not a deceleration from headline 2025, but an acceleration of the underlying business.

Section 02  ·  Why I Own It

First GAAP Profit. One Analyst. Invisible to Institutional Capital.

FY2025 was Bridger's first year of GAAP profitability — net income of $4.1M on $122.8M in revenue. Operating cash flow reached $16.7M, nearly double FY2024's $9.4M. The company has one sell-side analyst and does not screen into any major institutional mandate.

The asymmetry is not subtle. Bridger has compounded revenue at 38% annually since FY2022, reached GAAP profitability, generated $16.7M in operating cash flow, and holds a 100% historical contract renewal rate — all with one analyst covering it. The stock is uninvestable for a large cohort of potential holders not because the business is poor but because it is too small, too illiquid, and too thinly covered to justify institutional work. That changes when one more analyst initiates, when the preferred resolves, or when the market simply looks at what $45.3M in EBITDA looks like at a reasonable multiple.

Sam Davis was named permanent CEO in January 2025. His background is finance and accounting — he joined Bridger in 2019. In late 2024, Davis executed the most important capital event in Bridger's public history: a $331.5M refinancing that retired a restrictive municipal bond, lowered the borrowing rate, generated a $16.9M gain on the Bozeman campus sale-leaseback, and added a $100M Delayed Draw Term Loan — undrawn and available. The DDTL is the strategic weapon: it gives Davis the ability to acquire additional CL-415 aircraft without returning to equity markets. His words in January 2026: "I've never had that freedom. My fear was missing the window of opportunity, but now that's not hanging over my head." COO Bill Andrews came from Lockheed Martin. Incoming CFO Anne Hayes replaces Eric Gerratt, who is retiring.

The two Spanish scoopers were sourced through a partnership with MAB Funding LLC and brought into the operational fleet in 2025 after $14.0M in return-to-service work — now complete. Bridger also holds options on 2 additional airworthy CL-415s eligible for DDTL-funded acquisition with no equity dilution. The longer-term FF72 partnership — up to 20 ATR-72-based aerial firefighting aircraft announced at Bordeaux — represents optionality as that platform matures toward certification. Davis committed in January 2026 to expanding investor relations and attracting additional coverage in FY2026. Any second sell-side initiation mechanically expands the investable universe.

Supply Moat

Of the approximately 220 CL-415 and predecessor CL-215 variants ever produced before Bombardier closed the line in 2015, roughly 110 were the turbine-powered CL-415 variant. Approximately 12 of those 110 are in private hands. Bridger holds 8. The other 4 belong to Coulson Aviation, the only other US private scooper operator. The remaining ~98 turbine scoopers are government-owned — primarily European civil protection agencies and Canadian provinces — and are not for commercial hire.

De Havilland Canada's DHC-515 — the only announced successor to the CL-415 — had its forward fuselage structures joined in Calgary as of March 10, 2026, with no certification date or delivery timeline provided despite government orders from France, Greece, Morocco, Portugal, and Canada on the books. Davis said in January 2026 he does not expect the DHC-515 to be a practical solution before the 2030s. NAFA scooper pilot certification requires low-altitude and water-scooping training that takes years; the industry faces a projected 28,000-pilot gap by 2030. The moat window is conservatively 3 to 5 years.

Free Cash Flow Bridge (FY2025 Actuals)

The FCF question on BAER requires two clarifications most analysts miss. First, the preferred is payment-in-kind — it accretes non-cash, so there is no cash drain on operating FCF from the preferred. Second, FY2025 total capex of ~$50M was overwhelmingly growth capex (the two Spanish scoopers + new surveillance fleet). Maintenance capex — heavy maintenance checks, airworthiness directives, scheduled overhauls — runs approximately $7–8M per year on an 8-scooper fleet. Strip that out and the business already generates meaningful true FCF, with significant step-up potential as EBITDA scales.

Section 06  ·  Catalysts

Five Identifiable Events. Each Independently Sufficient.

None of these require heroic assumptions. The preferred resolution alone changes the entire equity math. The DDTL is fully available. The analyst coverage gap is one initiation away from closing.
1  ·  Preferred Equity Resolution

The $407.3M Series A preferred accretes at ~$27M per year and is held by Bain Capital and affiliated counterparties. A conversion, exchange, tender, or restructuring into common equity reduces net obligations immediately and changes the equity value per share dramatically. The 2024 refinancing — cooperative, not adversarial — already signals Bain's posture. Monitor 8-K filings. Any preferred modification is the single highest-impact event in the BAER equity story.

2  ·  Active Fire Season (Mean Reversion)

FY2025 produced 5.13M acres burned — 32% below the 20-year mean. Bridger generated record organic revenue of $108.8M in that environment. Mean reversion to the 20-year average adds incremental flight-hour revenue on top of a standby base that already set records. An above-average season above 8–9M acres — historically common — pushes EBITDA meaningfully above the author's base case.

3  ·  Additional Analyst Coverage

Davis committed in January 2026 to expanding investor relations and attracting additional coverage in FY2026. Any second initiation — from a firm with institutional distribution — opens the stock to managers who cannot own names with a single sell-side voice. Canaccord's existing $5.00 PT implies 143% upside. A second analyst initiating there reprices the conversation.

4  ·  DDTL-Funded Fleet Expansion

The $100M undrawn DDTL allows Davis to acquire additional CL-415 aircraft without equity dilution. Bridger holds options on 2 additional airworthy CL-415s. Each incremental scooper adds $6–8M in gross profit, immediately accretive. An acquisition announcement would be significant and unreplicable by a competitor without an existing fleet infrastructure.

5  ·  Aerial Firefighting Enhancement Act

The Aerial Firefighting Enhancement Act of 2025 enables the DoD to transfer surplus military aircraft to civilian operators at below-market cost — bipartisan legislation designed for operators in Bridger's position. The King Air's undisclosed DOD-adjacent mission suggests existing relationships that may facilitate access. Below-market aircraft acquisition via AFEA in FY2026 would be a material cost-basis advantage.

Section 07  ·  Risks

Three Legitimate Risks. One Requires Active Monitoring.

The preferred equity is the only risk that cannot resolve through the income statement alone. The other two are addressed by the contract structure and the appropriations data. Size accordingly.
Risk 1  ·  Preferred Impairs Common Equity

Section 10  ·  M&A Optionality

Acquisition Risk Is Lower Than the Market Assumes

One of the most common institutional questions about BAER is whether its government contracts would survive an acquisition. The answer resolves in BAER’s favor — and the analysis converts into a genuine optionality argument for the long thesis.

The Key Legal Fact: Stock Purchase = No Novation

Federal government contracts are governed by the Anti-Assignment Act (41 U.S.C. § 6305), which prohibits transfer of a government contract to a third party without explicit government approval. The mechanism for approval is a novation agreement under FAR Subpart 42.12 — a three-party agreement between the government, the seller, and the buyer, subject to broad contracting officer discretion.

Critical exception per FAR 42.1204(b): if an acquisition is structured as a stock purchase — buying BAER’s equity rather than its assets — no novation is required. Bridger Aerospace Group Holdings, Inc. remains the legal contracting party. The USFS and DOI relationships are undisturbed at the contract level. This is the standard structure for any serious acquirer of a public company.

What the Contracts Actually Require

BAER’s USFS contracts are aircraft-specific by FAA registration number (N-number) and serial number, with operator certification requirements including 14 CFR Part 137 (Agricultural Aircraft Operations), a 14 CFR Part 145 Repair Station Certificate, and Interagency Airtanker Board (IAB) approval for each aircraft. Critically, IAB certification is tied to the aircraft’s airworthiness configuration — not to the operator entity — meaning it survives an ownership change as long as the aircraft remain intact. There is no explicit change-of-control clause in the contract text. The government retains a termination-for-convenience right at any time, but this exists regardless of ownership and is not triggered by a change of control. The current National MATOC structure — a multi-vendor slot-based vehicle — is more durable across ownership changes than a single-vendor exclusive contract would be.

The practical obstacle to any acquisition is not government contracting mechanics — it is the preferred equity. Any acquirer must either retire the preferred at redemption value, negotiate a conversion with Bain Capital, or assume the accreting structure. At current prices (~$115M equity market cap), the preferred represents roughly 3.5x the common equity market cap. This is the real constraint on the acquirer universe, not contract transferability.
